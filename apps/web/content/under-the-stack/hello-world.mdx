## What It Does

You enter a URL. In 7-10 minutes, you get a forensic marketing audit that covers analytics tracking, ad pixel health, privacy and consent compliance, MarTech stack fragmentation, email security, SEO and traffic intelligence, accessibility, and dead software detection.

The scan runs a real browser session. Stealth fingerprints that are indistinguishable from a human visitor. Automatic bot wall detection and resolution. JavaScript execution, cookie auditing, consent manager probing, data layer inspection. This is not an HTTP request pretending to be an audit. Chloé, our pixel-art ghost, navigates your site the way a real user would.

What comes out:

- **MarketingIQ™ Score**: A single number (0-100) across 8 categories with traffic-light ratings
- **AI Executive Brief**: Strategic synthesis with a verdict headline, category assessments, and cross-cutting themes
- **PRD**: A technical remediation blueprint organized by priority, ready to hand to a dev team
- **Boss Deck**: 7 corporate-grade slides designed for executive presentations
- **Stack Analyzer**: Find the abandoned tools, the redundant pairs, and get a lean vs. optimal recommendation
- **GhostChat™**: An AI assistant that absorbed your entire scan for follow-up questions
- **PDF Export + .MD for NotebookLM**: Share as a document or let Google's AI narrate your audit

What agencies charge $3K+ for and deliver in weeks, this delivers in minutes. The full engineering story behind how it works is in [How I Built a Ghost That Audits Your Marketing Stack](/blog/how-i-built-alphascan).

## How the Design Was Born

I didn't want another white SaaS dashboard with blue buttons and a sans-serif font. Every audit tool in the marketing space looks exactly the same: clean, inoffensive, forgettable. I wanted something with a point of view.

So I ran a design ideation session with AI, roleplaying a [MSCHF](https://mschf.com/)-style agency engagement. The AI pitched its first brand direction. It was generic, safe references to Apple and Airbnb, the kind of aesthetic that could be any product from any decade. I rejected it and asked for someone who actually understood what 2026 looks like, not what 2018 looked like.

The AI course-corrected. Hard:

> *"Throws the Warby Parkers in the Hudson (from the previous account director). You're absolutely right. That was tragic. It's 2026. The era of the 'Millennial agency creative director' in their Dumbo loft is dead. I'm 28. I live in Greenpoint, but I spend half my year in Berlin or Tokyo. I cut my teeth working for MSCHF, moved to a boutique branding studio that strictly works with post-AI SaaS companies and luxury fashion houses, and I don't wear glasses because I have 20/20 vision and a mild obsession with biohacking. I wear vintage Issey Miyake and brutalist silver jewelry."*

From that character came references to A24 films, Ghost in the Shell, early 2000s hacking culture, and zero-trust computing interfaces mixed with high-fashion editorial. That session shaped every design decision in AlphaScan.

The pixel-art ghost named Chloé who floats around your screen and fires rainbow laser beams at UI elements. The retro desktop OS with draggable windows, a taskbar, and desktop icons. The GhostScan™ sequence with matrix rain, terminal boot sequences, and 4 channels of ASCII cinema (one of them is a Rick Roll). A Minesweeper clone called Ghost Sweeper that has a jumpscare when you hit a mine. A 4-channel ASCII TV you can watch while waiting for results.

[MSCHF](https://mschf.com/) was the direct inspiration. They run [ascii.theater](https://ascii.theater/), a full ASCII movie streaming service. They embed unexpected, delightful things inside their products. I wanted that same energy in a tool that actually does something useful. Every "unnecessary" detail exists because it makes people screenshot the app and send it to a friend instead of closing the tab.

Creative direction is knowing what you don't want and pushing until you arrive at what you do.

## Why Everything Is Pink

The pink is a data-driven decision, not a personal preference.

We researched the target market for marketing professionals in the US and Puerto Rico. The data: the audience skews heavily female and culturally progressive. When approximately 70% of your users are women, you design for that audience.

So the palette went pink. But not the pastel corporate pink you see on wellness apps. A dark void pink that functions as a black replacement. The palette ramps from near-achromatic darks into saturated pink gradually. It reads as moody and serious, not playful. Dark enough for long working sessions, distinctive enough that you know exactly what app you're looking at from across the room.

Then there's the Boss Deck. Same data, completely different visual language. Blue and gold. Corporate styling. Zero pink. The Boss Deck is designed for the executive who receives the report, the VP, the CMO, the CEO. The data needs to look like strategic intelligence from a consulting engagement, not a colorful consumer product. If the design distracts from the findings, the design failed.

Two audiences. Two visual systems. One product.
