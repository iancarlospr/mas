# How I Built a Ghost That Audits Your Marketing Stack

This is the story of how a broken tracking pixel, a lot of stubborn engineering, and a pixel-art ghost with laser eyes became a product that audits your entire marketing stack in minutes.

I'm going to be honest with you. This isn't a polished founder narrative. There's no moment where I stood on a mountaintop and saw the future of martech. There's a moment where I watched a company bleed money for three months because nobody could see a broken pixel, and I got angry enough to build something about it.

## The Moment

I watched a company pay $8,000 a month to an agency that couldn't tell them their Meta pixel had stopped firing after a site redesign.

That pixel was responsible for tracking $200K in annual attributed revenue. It broke during a routine deploy. Nobody noticed. Not the agency running their paid campaigns. Not the internal marketing team reviewing dashboards every Monday. Not the ad platform itself, which just quietly stopped receiving conversion data and moved on with its life.

The pixel was broken for three months.

During those three months, the team kept optimizing campaigns against incomplete data. They wondered why ROAS was declining. They tested new creative. They adjusted audiences. They had three rounds of meetings about "the performance dip." The agency recommended increasing budget to compensate for what looked like declining efficiency.

They spent more money to fix a problem that wasn't a strategy problem. It was a single line of JavaScript that stopped executing on the confirmation page after someone refactored the checkout flow. That's it. One broken event listener. Three months. Tens of thousands in wasted optimization against phantom data.

When someone finally found it, by accident, while debugging something else, the reaction wasn't relief. It was this sick, quiet realization: *how many other things are broken that we can't see?*

That question wouldn't leave me alone.

## The First Prototype

The first version of AlphaScan was embarrassing. I'm talking about a Node script that made HTTP requests, parsed response headers, and checked for the presence of common tracking pixels in the HTML source. It ran in a terminal. The output was a JSON blob. It took about 4 seconds.

But here's the thing: I pointed it at a real website, a friend's e-commerce store, and within those 4 seconds it found that her Google Analytics consent mode was misconfigured, her DMARC record was set to `none` (meaning anyone could spoof emails from her domain), and she had three abandoned tracking scripts still loading on every page view.

She'd been paying an agency $2,500 a month for eight months. Nobody had flagged any of it.

That was the moment. Not a grand vision moment. A "wait, this actually works and nobody else is doing it" moment.

So I kept going. The script that checked headers became a script that checked 8 things. Then 15. Then I hit the wall that every developer hits when they try to audit modern websites: you can't see anything interesting from a static HTTP response. Modern sites are JavaScript applications. The tracking pixels, the consent flows, the tag manager configurations, none of that exists in the initial HTML. You need a real browser.

So I built a browser-based scanner. Then I needed modules that could run independently without crashing each other. Then I needed AI to synthesize 45 modules of raw data into something a human could actually act on. Then I needed a UI that didn't make people fall asleep while waiting for results.

Four seconds became ninety seconds. One JSON blob became 45 specialized analysis modules across 8 scoring categories. A terminal script became a full engine with job queues, browser automation, bot detection handling, and multi-model AI synthesis.

The scope crept. But the anger stayed the same: this information should not be invisible. And it definitely shouldn't cost $15,000 and take six weeks to discover.

## The Hard Parts

Building this thing nearly broke me at least four times. Here are the four walls I hit hardest.

### Making a Browser That Doesn't Get Caught

Here's something most people don't know: websites can detect automated browsers. The moment you spin up a headless browser to scan a site, half the internet's bot protection systems flag you as suspicious. Cloudflare, Akamai, PerimeterX. They're all watching. Your scan gets blocked, your data comes back empty, and you've built an audit tool that can't actually audit anything.

So I had to build a browser that looks and acts human. Consistent fingerprints: user agent, GPU renderer, screen resolution, timezone, hardware specs, all internally consistent so nothing looks fabricated. The browser handles bot protection challenges automatically. It navigates with realistic referrers. It passes the same detection tests that catch every off-the-shelf automation tool.

This was months of work. Months of "it works on 90% of sites" followed by weeks of debugging why the other 10% served a blank challenge page. Every time a bot protection provider updates their detection, something breaks and I'm back in the weeds reverse-engineering what changed.

It's an arms race, and it never ends. But it's the foundation everything else sits on. If you can't get past the front door, nothing else matters.

### 45 Modules That Can't Crash Each Other

AlphaScan runs 45 analysis modules on every scan. Some make HTTP requests. Some need a live browser page. Some call external market data APIs. Some run AI models. They execute in five sequential phases: passive checks, browser-based analysis, deep interaction scans, external data lookups, and AI synthesis.

The hard constraint: **one module failing can never take down another module**. If the accessibility checker times out, the SEO analysis still needs to complete. If an external API is down, the browser-based modules that already ran can't lose their data. Everything has to degrade gracefully.

Every module runs inside its own error boundary with independent retry logic and timeout limits. If a module fails after all retries, it stores an error result and the system moves on. No cascading failures. No stalled scans. The user might get 43 out of 45 modules instead of 45, but they never get zero because one API had a bad day.

This sounds straightforward on paper. In practice, it meant designing an execution framework where modules are completely isolated but still share context (the same browser page, the same collected data) without stepping on each other. It was the most tedious engineering work I've ever done, and also the most important.

### AI That Actually Thinks

Here's what happens when you send raw audit data to an AI model: you get your data back, restated in slightly different words, with the word "comprehensive" used fourteen times.

That's not analysis. That's a summarizer wearing a lab coat.

Getting AI to produce genuine strategic synthesis, the kind of insight a $300/hour consultant would give you, required building structured rubrics, evaluation frameworks, and a multi-model pipeline. Fast models handle the data aggregation and pattern recognition. Slower, more capable models handle the strategic reasoning and cross-module synthesis. Quality validation catches outputs that are vague, repetitive, or just restating inputs.

The AI Executive Brief isn't generated from a template with your numbers dropped in. It reads every module result, identifies cross-cutting themes (your consent issues are connected to your analytics gaps which are connected to your attribution problems), and produces a strategic narrative with a verdict headline, category-level assessments, and prioritized recommendations with dollar estimates.

It took dozens of iterations to get the prompts right. Dozens more to build the quality checks that catch bad outputs. And I'm still tuning it. But when it works, when the AI connects three seemingly unrelated findings into a coherent strategic insight, it produces analysis that would take a human consultant days to assemble.

### A Desktop OS for an Audit Tool (Why Though?)

This is the one that makes other developers look at me funny. You built a retro desktop operating system... as the UI for a marketing audit tool?

Yes. Here's why.

Marketing audits are boring. Dashboards are boring. Every SaaS tool in the marketing space looks exactly the same: white background, blue accent color, sidebar navigation, table of results with red/yellow/green indicators. They're built by enterprise design systems that optimize for "inoffensive" and land on "forgettable."

We wanted AlphaScan to make you feel something. Not "this is a useful tool." People say that about spreadsheets. We wanted "wait, what is this, I need to show someone." The kind of reaction that makes you text a screenshot to a friend.

So the whole app is a desktop OS. Draggable windows with title bars. A taskbar. Desktop icons. Your scan results open in a maximized window. Your payment confirmation is a dialog box. There's a Minesweeper clone called Ghost Sweeper (it has a jumpscare when you hit a mine). There's a 4-channel ASCII TV you can watch while your scan runs.

It's dumb and it's extra and it works. People stay. People explore. People run a second scan just to watch the GhostScan sequence again: the matrix rain, the terminal boot, the ASCII movies. Engagement metrics that enterprise UX would kill for, built by making people smile instead of optimizing funnel conversion rates.

## The Ghost

Chloé started as a favicon. A tiny ghost icon in a browser tab. Then she became a pixel-art sprite on the page. Then she started floating around the screen. Then she got laser eyes. Then she started watching ASCII movies. Then she developed strong opinions about your DMARC policy.

She exists because marketing is already full of bland dashboards that say "Action Required" in gray Helvetica. Nobody needs another neutral, corporate-voiced tool that speaks in passive voice and hedges every recommendation with "consider potentially exploring the possibility of..."

Chloé doesn't hedge. She tells you your consent banner is a liability, babe. She tells you that abandoned Hotjar script is slowing down your site and nobody's logged into it since 2023. She's sassy, she's blunt, and she delivers bad news with the energy of a best friend who cares enough to be honest.

She's rendered on a 32x42 pixel canvas with hand-coded pixel art. She has 9 emotional states: idle, scanning, smug, celebrating, sleeping, and a few others. Her bedsheet bottom has wavy scalloped tails that animate on a frame cycle. She fires rainbow laser beams from her eyes at UI elements. She floats around the desktop on a gentle sine wave and occasionally darts across the screen for no reason.

Building a pixel ghost with emotions and laser eyes is objectively not the most important engineering work on this project. But it might be the most important design decision. Because Chloé is the reason people remember AlphaScan after they close the tab. She's the personality layer that turns "marketing audit tool" into "that ghost app."

Marketing tools don't need more features. They need a soul.

## Building from Puerto Rico, For the World

I'm building this from an island in the Caribbean. No VC. No team of 50. No San Francisco co-working space with $12 cold brew and a mandatory stand-up at 9 AM.

There's a desk, a good monitor, trade winds through the window, and coquís singing at night. The palm trees don't help with debugging. But they help with perspective.

Building indie means every decision is a trade-off against time and money. No A/B testing team. No dedicated DevOps person. No design system committee. Just ship, measure, fix, ship again. The velocity is high because the feedback loop is short and there's nobody to schedule a meeting with before pushing a change.

It also means building for the world from day one. AlphaScan isn't a Bay Area product. It's built on an island for marketers in Miami, Mexico City, London, São Paulo, Singapore, anywhere someone is running ads, tracking conversions, and wondering if their tools actually work. Marketing is global. The anxiety about whether your tracking is broken is universal.

The indie maker path is not glamorous. There are weeks where the hardest bug is also the loneliest bug, because there's no one to rubber-duck with at 2 AM. But there's something about building a product with this level of care, this level of obsession, this close to the ocean. It shows up in the work. You build different when every feature, every pixel, every interaction is a choice you made deliberately, not a ticket someone assigned.
