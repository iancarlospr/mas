# Best Website Audit Tools in 2026 (Honest Comparison)

Here's something nobody in this space wants to say out loud: **there is no single tool that does everything.** Not Lighthouse. Not SEMrush. Not us. Every audit tool was built to answer a specific set of questions, and the real skill is knowing which tool to reach for and when.

This post is not a "why AlphaScan is the best" puff piece. We're going to walk through the major website audit tools available in 2026: what they're genuinely great at, where they fall short, and who should actually be using them. We'll include ourselves in the comparison, warts and all, because if we're going to ask you to trust us with your marketing audit, the least we can do is be straight with you about what we can and can't do.

Different tools. Different jobs. Let's get into it.

## The Tools

### Google Lighthouse / PageSpeed Insights

Lighthouse is the baseline. It's free, it's built into Chrome DevTools, and if you've ever right-clicked "Inspect" on a webpage, you're halfway there. PageSpeed Insights is the hosted version that runs Lighthouse against real-world Chrome User Experience Report (CrUX) data.

**What it does well:**

- **Core Web Vitals**: LCP, CLS, INP. This is the gold standard for performance measurement.
- **Accessibility basics**: contrast ratios, alt text, ARIA roles, focus management. Solid automated a11y checks.
- **SEO fundamentals**: meta tags, crawlability, mobile viewport, robots.txt.
- **PWA checks**: service worker, manifest, offline capability.
- **Best practices**: HTTPS, image aspect ratios, deprecated APIs.

**Where it falls short:**

Lighthouse checks whether your page loads fast. It does not check whether your marketing works. There's no analytics verification, no pixel audit, no consent compliance check, no MarTech mapping, no competitive intelligence, and no evaluation of whether the 14 third-party scripts on your page are actually doing their jobs.

It also runs in a synthetic environment, a simulated mobile device with throttled CPU and network. That's great for consistent benchmarking, but it doesn't capture what happens in a real browser session with cookies, consent banners, and dynamic tag managers firing.

- **Price:** Free
- **Best for:** Developers and engineers checking page performance and basic web standards.

Great at what it does. But it checks maybe 5% of what a full marketing audit covers. If your analytics are silently dropping 30% of your conversions, Lighthouse will give you a 98 and a green checkmark.

### Screaming Frog SEO Spider

If you do SEO professionally, you probably already have Screaming Frog open in another tab. It's been the workhorse of technical SEO for over a decade, and for good reason. Nothing else crawls a site with this level of granularity.

**What it does well:**

- **Deep crawling**: will spider 10,000+ URLs, map your entire site architecture, and find every broken link, redirect chain, and orphan page.
- **On-page SEO**: title tags, meta descriptions, H1s, canonical tags, hreflang, structured data.
- **Sitemap analysis**: validates XML sitemaps against actual crawl data.
- **Log file analysis**: cross-reference crawl data with server logs to see what Googlebot actually hits.
- **Custom extraction**: XPath/CSS/regex extraction from any page element.

**Where it falls short:**

Screaming Frog is a crawler, not a browser. It sends HTTP requests and parses HTML. It doesn't execute JavaScript, doesn't render SPAs, doesn't interact with consent banners, and doesn't see what happens after a user accepts cookies. In 2026, where the average marketing site loads half its tracking stack dynamically via a tag manager, that's a significant blind spot.

No analytics verification. No pixel auditing. No consent mode validation. No MarTech evaluation. No competitive intelligence. It's the best at what it does, but what it does is technical SEO crawling, not marketing auditing.

- **Price:** Free (500 URL limit) / £199/year for the full version
- **Best for:** SEO specialists and agencies doing site migrations, technical audits, and large-scale crawl analysis.

### SEMrush Site Audit

SEMrush is the Swiss Army knife of SEO platforms, and their Site Audit tool is one of the better automated SEO health checks available. If your team already has a SEMrush subscription, you're probably already running it.

**What it does well:**

- **Crawl health**: broken links, redirect chains, slow pages, duplicate content, thin content.
- **Core Web Vitals integration**: pulls CrUX data alongside crawl findings.
- **Internal linking analysis**: link depth, link equity distribution, orphan pages.
- **Scheduled monitoring**: re-crawl on a schedule and track issues over time.
- **Thematic reports**: groups issues by category (crawlability, HTTPS, performance, etc.)

**Where it falls short:**

SEMrush Site Audit is still fundamentally an SEO tool. It doesn't open a real browser. It doesn't verify whether your Google Analytics 4 is actually collecting data. It doesn't check if your Meta pixel is firing on the right events. It doesn't validate consent mode configuration. It doesn't map your MarTech stack or tell you which tools are redundant or abandoned.

The pricing also puts it out of reach for freelancers and small business owners who just need to audit one site. You're paying for an entire SEO platform to access the audit feature.

- **Price:** $139.95/month (Pro) / $249.95/month (Guru). Site Audit included in all plans
- **Best for:** SEO teams and agencies that already live inside SEMrush and want ongoing crawl monitoring.

### Ahrefs Site Audit

Similar story to SEMrush. Ahrefs built their reputation on backlink analysis and keyword research, and their Site Audit tool is a solid companion to the SEO workflow.

**What it does well:**

- **Technical SEO crawling**: similar coverage to SEMrush: broken links, redirects, canonicals, meta tags.
- **JavaScript rendering**: Ahrefs does render JavaScript during crawls, which is a meaningful advantage over tools that only parse raw HTML.
- **Performance metrics**: page load time, resource size, CWV integration.
- **Clean UI**: the issue categorization and severity scoring is genuinely well-designed.

**Where it falls short:**

Same fundamental gap as SEMrush: it's an SEO audit, not a marketing audit. No analytics verification, no pixel checks, no consent compliance, no MarTech mapping, no competitive stack analysis. The JavaScript rendering is a nice touch for catching SPA-related SEO issues, but it's not the same as a full browser session that interacts with the page the way a real visitor would.

- **Price:** $99/month (Lite) / $199/month (Standard)
- **Best for:** SEO professionals and content teams invested in the Ahrefs ecosystem.

### HubSpot Website Grader

HubSpot's Website Grader is the audit tool you send to your boss when they ask "how's our website doing?" It's free, it's pretty, and it gives you a score out of 100 in about 10 seconds. That's both its strength and its limitation.

**What it does well:**

- **Speed**: enter a URL, get a score. No setup, no crawling, no waiting.
- **Accessible presentation**: the results are designed for non-technical stakeholders. Clean, visual, easy to understand.
- **Surface checks**: performance basics, SEO presence (meta descriptions, headings), mobile responsiveness, basic security (HTTPS).

**Where it falls short:**

Honestly? Almost everything beyond the surface. It checks if you have a meta description, but it doesn't check if your meta description is any good. It checks if your site loads, but it doesn't check if your tracking loads with it. There's no analytics audit, no pixel verification, no consent compliance, no MarTech evaluation, no competitive data.

The score is also... generous. We've seen sites with broken analytics, missing consent banners, and critical security misconfigurations score above 80. That's not a grading tool; that's a participation trophy.

- **Price:** Free
- **Best for:** Getting a quick gut-check to share with non-technical stakeholders. Not for actual decision-making.

It checks if you have a meta description and calls it a day.

### Wappalyzer / BuiltWith

These are technology detection tools, and they're useful for exactly that: figuring out what's installed on a website. Wappalyzer shows you the CMS, analytics tools, ad tech, frameworks, and third-party services running on any site. BuiltWith does the same with more granular historical data.

**What they do well:**

- **Tech stack detection**: accurate identification of hundreds of technologies: analytics platforms, tag managers, CDNs, A/B testing tools, chat widgets, CRMs, ad pixels, payment processors.
- **Competitive research**: see what tech your competitors use. BuiltWith's historical data lets you see what they added and removed over time.
- **Lead generation**: BuiltWith in particular is popular for building prospecting lists filtered by technology usage.

**Where they fall short:**

Detection is not evaluation. Wappalyzer will tell you that GA4 is installed. It will not tell you whether GA4 is configured correctly, whether it's collecting the right events, whether consent mode is properly wired up, or whether half your traffic is being silently excluded due to a misconfigured data stream.

Knowing what's installed is step one. Knowing whether it works is the actual audit.

- **Price:** Wappalyzer: Free (basic browser extension) / paid tiers for API and bulk lookups. BuiltWith: Free (basic) / from $295/month for pro features.
- **Best for:** Sales teams prospecting by technology, and marketers doing initial competitive stack research.

They tell you what's installed. They don't tell you if it's working.

## When to Use What

These aren't competing tools. They're different tools for different jobs. Here's when to reach for each one:

**Use Lighthouse** when you need to benchmark page performance, chase Core Web Vitals, or verify accessibility basics before a deploy. It's free, it's in your browser, and it's the standard.

**Use Screaming Frog** when you need to crawl an entire site: site migrations, broken link sweeps, redirect audits, hreflang validation, sitemap cross-checks. Nothing else crawls like it.

**Use SEMrush or Ahrefs** when you need an ongoing SEO platform: keyword research, rank tracking, backlink analysis, and scheduled site audits. If you're doing SEO professionally, you probably need one of these.

**Use HubSpot Website Grader** when you need a quick, shareable snapshot for a non-technical stakeholder. It's the "before" photo. Just don't make decisions based on it.

**Use Wappalyzer or BuiltWith** when you need to know what technology is running on a site, yours or a competitor's. Great for initial reconnaissance. Just remember: detection is not evaluation.
